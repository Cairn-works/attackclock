The Problem

Guidance has been published -- but it focuses on the manufacturer, not the product.

Guidance Published, Gap Remains

On March 20, 2026, DoC/DHS published Annex A guidance for Conditional Approval. It covers three disclosure categories: corporate structure, supply chain geography, and onshoring commitments. Technical security assessment of the product itself is not currently part of the process.

125 Million Foreign Routers

An estimated 125 million consumer routers in US households are manufactured overseas -- primarily Taiwan and China. ASUS, D-Link, Ubiquiti, Synology, TP-Link. No new models can enter the market without Conditional Approval.

The Threat Is Documented

Volt Typhoon maintained access to US critical infrastructure for 5+ years via compromised SOHO routers. Flax Typhoon controlled 260,000 devices. Salt Typhoon penetrated telecom providers. These are not hypotheticals.

Manufacturer Trust, Not Product Trust

The current guidance establishes trust in the manufacturer -- who they are, where they build, whether they will onshore. It does not establish trust in the product -- whether the firmware is secure, the credentials are hardened, or the patches ship on time.

Completing the Picture

The Conditional Approval process establishes trust in the manufacturer. VCRI establishes trust in the product. Together, they complete the picture.

The Conditional Approval process addresses three critical questions: Who makes this router? Where is it made? Will they manufacture in the US?

These are essential -- but they address the manufacturer, not the product.

A fourth question remains: Is the router firmware actually secure? VCRI answers that question.

Guidance Details

Annex A disclosure requirements published March 20, 2026 -- three categories of manufacturer information.

Corporate Structure

  • Ownership and control relationships
  • Foreign ownership, control, or influence (FOCI)
  • Board member nationalities
  • Government affiliations or obligations

Supply Chain

  • Bill of materials and component origins
  • Geographic concentration of suppliers
  • Single points of failure (SPOFs)
  • Subcontractor and OEM relationships

Onshoring Commitment

  • Time-bound manufacturing transition plan
  • Quarterly progress updates
  • Capital expenditure commitments
  • US facility and workforce plans
Key terms: Approvals are granted for 18-month periods. Zero Conditional Approvals have been issued to date. An officer of the company must certify all disclosures under penalty of perjury.

The VCRI Solution

A standardized, evidence-based assessment methodology built on established frameworks.

CyberAssuranceMatrix (CAM)

6 TIPPSS security dimensions, 6 asset categories, 5 CMMI maturity levels. A 180-cell scoring framework that defines what "does not pose unacceptable risks" actually means for a router.

808

Router-Relevant Controls

808 controls identified as relevant to consumer router security. 43 classified as critical. Mapped through the Secure Controls Framework for vendor-agnostic comparison.

Dollar-at-Risk (VCAR)

VCAR quantifies risk in dollar terms: Process Value multiplied by Industry Incident Duration. Gives DoC/DHS a way to prioritize applications by measured risk, not guesswork.

Continuous Monitoring

Not point-in-time certification. VCRI continuously ingests vendor security telemetry. A router that was secure at approval time but degrades later gets flagged automatically.

Independent Nonprofit

VCRI is a 501(c)(3) with no commercial interest in outcomes. The scoring is independent of any vendor relationship. Evidence-based, not self-attestation.

How It Works

Four steps from firmware analysis to Conditional Approval application.

01

Firmware Analysis Generates Evidence

Manufacturer's firmware analysis platform (Finite State, Eclypsium, Binarly, or others) generates SBOMs, VEX documents, and firmware integrity evidence. Manufacturer authorizes VCRI read-only API access.

02

VCRI Independently Scores Against CAM

CRIBL-based pipeline normalizes all vendor tool data into the TIPPSS schema. SCF control mapping, maturity classification, and sensitivity redaction produce a standardized CAM score.

03

Gap Analysis with Remediation Roadmap

VCRI identifies which TIPPSS dimensions fall below minimum thresholds and provides a prioritized remediation roadmap. Manufacturers know exactly what to fix and in what order.

04

Assessment Package for Conditional Approval

Complete VCRI assessment -- CAM scores, VCAR risk quantification, gap analysis, and remediation status -- submitted alongside the manufacturer's Conditional Approval application to DoC/DHS.

Minimum Maturity Thresholds

Proposed CMMI maturity levels for Conditional Approval eligibility across TIPPSS dimensions.

TIPPSS Dimension Minimum Level What It Means for Routers
Trust (Provenance) L3 - Defined Vendor-attested SBOMs with documented supply chain. Hardware and firmware provenance is verifiable.
Identity (Device Auth) L3 - Defined Certificate-based device identity, not shared credentials. Addresses Typhoon-pattern attacks.
Privacy (Data Handling) L2 - Managed Basic data minimization and encryption. No unauthorized exfiltration of DNS or traffic data.
Protection (Hardening) L3 - Defined Organization-wide vulnerability management in CI/CD. Firmware resistant to known attack patterns.
Safety (Fail-Safe) L2 - Managed Documented recovery procedures. Router fails into known-safe state, not persistent threat.
Security (Integrity) L3 - Defined Code signing, secure boot, integrity monitoring. Firmware updates are authenticated.

L3 across Trust, Identity, Protection, and Security with L2 in Privacy and Safety represents a defensible minimum. Below L3 in any critical dimension indicates unacceptable risk for Conditional Approval.
